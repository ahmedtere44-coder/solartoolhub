Picking an inverter size is where a lot of DIY solar and backup-power plans go wrong. It's tempting to just add up the wattage printed on your appliances and buy an inverter that covers the total — but that number alone can leave you with an inverter that trips or shuts down the moment your refrigerator or air conditioner switches on.

Running watts vs. starting watts

Every electrical load falls into one of two categories:

  • Resistive loads — things like lighting, TVs, laptops, and electric heaters. They draw a steady amount of power with no significant spike when switched on.
  • Motor-driven loads — refrigerators, air conditioners, washing machines, and water pumps. These draw a short burst of extra current — often 2 to 3 times their normal running wattage — for a second or two while the motor spins up. This is called the starting watts or surge watts.

An inverter has to be sized for the highest combined demand it will ever face at once — which usually happens when your biggest motor-driven appliance starts up while everything else is already running. That's exactly what the calculator above accounts for: it adds up your steady running load, then adds the extra surge from whichever single appliance has the largest starting spike.

Grid-tied vs. off-grid inverter sizing

This calculator is built around off-grid and hybrid backup scenarios, where the inverter has to directly power your appliances from batteries. If you're planning a standard grid-tied solar system with no battery backup, the inverter is sized differently — based on your solar array's total output (see our panel calculator), not your appliance load, since the grid handles demand spikes for you automatically.

Field note

On one residential project, an engineering oversight left a 2-ton air conditioner out of the load calculation before the system was wired up — the inverter was pushed well beyond its rated capacity and failed. I've seen this exact mistake happen more than once: poor load estimation before connecting the system is one of the most common causes of inverter failure in the field, and it's almost always avoidable with a proper checklist like the one above.

Why the 20% safety margin matters

Even after accounting for starting surge, sizing an inverter right at your calculated peak leaves no room for error. A 20% buffer above your calculated peak load is standard practice for a few reasons:

  • Appliance nameplate wattage is often a rough figure, and real-world draw can run slightly higher, especially on older or poorly maintained motors.
  • Running an inverter consistently near 100% of its rated capacity shortens its lifespan and increases heat buildup.
  • It leaves headroom to add a small appliance later without needing to replace the whole inverter.

This is why the calculator rounds your peak requirement up by 20% before matching it to the nearest standard inverter size.

Pure sine wave vs. modified sine wave

Inverter sizing isn't just about wattage — the waveform type matters just as much for certain appliances:

Inverter typeBest suited for
Pure sine waveMotors, refrigerators, air conditioners, sensitive electronics, medical equipment — anything with a compressor or precision circuitry
Modified sine waveBasic resistive loads like simple lighting or heating elements, where cost matters more than efficiency

Running a motor-driven appliance on a modified sine wave inverter often causes it to run hotter, less efficiently, and with a shorter lifespan — sometimes it won't start at all. For any home system with a refrigerator or air conditioner, a pure sine wave inverter is worth the extra cost.

A worked example

Take the default selections in the calculator above: a refrigerator (150W, ×3 surge), an air conditioner (1200W, ×3 surge), 8 LED bulbs (10W each), a TV (100W), and two phone chargers (60W each). Running watts add up to 150 + 1200 + 80 + 100 + 120 = 1,650W. The air conditioner has the largest surge addition (1200W × 2 extra = 2,400W), so peak load is 1,650 + 2,400 = 4,050W. Adding the 20% margin brings that to 4,860W, which rounds up to a 5,000W inverter — exactly what the calculator returns.

Frequently asked questions

Can I run two motor-driven appliances at the same time on this sizing?

Yes, as long as they don't both start at the exact same instant. The calculator assumes your other motor-driven appliances are already running steadily (contributing only their running watts) when the single largest one starts up. If you frequently start two large motors simultaneously — like an AC and a water pump — size up further for extra headroom.

Do solar panels need to match the inverter size exactly?

No. Inverter size is based on your load (what you're powering), while panel count is based on your energy generation needs. The two are related but sized independently — check our panel calculator for the generation side of the equation.

What's the difference between an inverter's continuous and surge rating?

The continuous (or running) rating is what the inverter can sustain indefinitely. The surge rating — usually listed separately by manufacturers, often double the continuous rating for a few seconds — is what it can handle briefly during motor start-up. When comparing inverters, check both numbers against your calculated running and peak loads.

Does inverter efficiency affect how much battery or panel capacity I need?

Yes. Inverters aren't 100% efficient — typically 90–95% for a good pure sine wave unit — meaning a small percentage of the power drawn from your battery is lost as heat during conversion from DC to AC. When sizing your battery bank or panel array, add this loss on top of your appliance load rather than assuming a 1:1 conversion.

Can I oversize my inverter without any downside?

A larger inverter gives you more headroom, but it's not free of trade-offs. Bigger inverters cost more upfront, and most units run less efficiently at very low load — so an oversized inverter powering just a few small appliances may waste more energy on standby draw than a correctly sized one would.
