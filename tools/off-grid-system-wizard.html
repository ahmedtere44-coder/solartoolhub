Sizing an off-grid solar system by hand means juggling four interconnected calculations at once — and a mistake in the first one (energy use) throws off every calculation that follows it. This wizard walks through the same logic a designer would use, in order, so each component is sized against your actual numbers rather than a generic assumption.

The sizing logic, step by step

  1. Daily energy use sets the foundation — it determines how much the panel array needs to generate and how much the battery bank needs to store.
  2. Peak simultaneous load is independent of daily energy — a system can have modest daily use but a high peak if several things run at once, and that peak is what the inverter has to survive.
  3. Sun hours and battery autonomy together determine how oversized the panel array and battery bank need to be to comfortably ride out real-world weather, not just a perfect sunny day.
  4. Panel array size then drives the charge controller size, since the controller has to handle the full array's output current.

How each number is calculated

ComponentFormula
Panel array (W)Daily Wh ÷ (peak sun hours × 0.75 system efficiency)
Battery bank (Ah)(Daily Wh × autonomy days) ÷ (depth of discharge × battery voltage)
Inverter (W)Peak simultaneous load × 1.25 safety margin, rounded to a standard size
Charge controller (A)(Panel array W ÷ battery voltage) × 1.25 safety margin, rounded to a standard size

The 0.75 system efficiency factor for the panel array accounts for real-world losses — wiring resistance, temperature effects, dust, and inverter/charge controller conversion losses — that always separate a panel's rated wattage from what actually reaches your battery bank.

What this wizard doesn't replace

  • Site-specific shading and orientation analysis — sun hours here are a regional average, not a measurement of your specific roof or ground-mount location.
  • Local code requirements — grounding, disconnects, and wiring methods still need to follow your local electrical code regardless of what this wizard recommends.
  • Professional design review for larger systems — this is a solid starting point for planning and budgeting, not a substitute for a licensed designer's sign-off on a system that will be permitted or inspected.

To fine-tune any single part of the recommendation, use the dedicated tools: daily load, panel, battery, charge controller, and inverter calculators. For background on system types, see our grid-tied vs off-grid vs hybrid guide.

Frequently asked questions

Why does battery type change the recommended capacity so much?

Depth of discharge is the practical reason — a lead-acid battery bank needs roughly 60% more total capacity than an equivalent LiFePO4 bank to deliver the same usable energy, since only about half of a lead-acid bank's capacity can be used regularly without shortening its life.

Should I size for my average day or my worst day?

For an off-grid system, size for your highest-usage season and factor in realistic autonomy days for your local weather patterns — designing around an unusually good day is the most common way off-grid systems end up under-sized.

Can I use this for a grid-tied hybrid system too?

The panel and inverter math still applies, but battery autonomy matters less on a hybrid system since the grid can cover gaps — you may reasonably choose fewer autonomy days than a true off-grid design would need.
