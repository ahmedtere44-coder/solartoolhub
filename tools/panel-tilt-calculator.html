Panel tilt angle is one of the few completely free performance gains in a solar system — get it right and you can pick up meaningfully more annual output from the exact same panels, with zero extra hardware cost on a fixed-mount system. This calculator gives you three angle options depending on what you're optimizing for.

The basic rule of thumb

For a fixed panel angle optimized across the whole year, the long-standing rule of thumb is: tilt angle ≈ your location's latitude. A location at 32° latitude gets close to its best year-round average output with panels tilted at roughly 32° from horizontal.

Why winter and summer angles differ

The sun sits lower in the sky during winter and higher during summer. If you want to squeeze out extra production in one season at the expense of the other, the standard adjustments are:

  • Winter-optimized: latitude + 15° — a steeper angle that faces the panels more directly into the lower winter sun path.
  • Summer-optimized: latitude − 15° — a flatter angle that matches the sun's higher summer arc.

This matters most for off-grid systems where winter production is often the bottleneck for the whole year's design — in that case, biasing toward the winter angle (or building in seasonal adjustability) can matter more than maximizing the annual total.

Other factors that override the pure math

  • Roof pitch. On a fixed roof, you're often working with whatever angle the roof already has — repositioning panels to a completely different angle usually isn't practical or cost-effective for typical rooftop installs.
  • Snow shedding. In snowy climates, a steeper angle than the pure-math optimum helps snow slide off instead of blocking the array for days after a storm.
  • Wind loading. Steeper tilt angles catch more wind — racking and mounting hardware need to be rated for the angle you choose, especially on open ground-mount arrays.

Frequently asked questions

Does the facing direction matter as much as the angle?

Generally, yes — a panel facing directly toward the equator (south in the northern hemisphere, north in the southern) at a moderately wrong angle will usually outperform a panel at the perfect angle but facing the wrong direction. Get the direction right first.

What if my roof angle doesn't match the calculated optimum?

Being off by 10-15° from the ideal angle typically costs only a few percent of annual output — it's rarely worth a costly re-angling structure just to chase the last couple of percent on a standard rooftop system.

Should I adjust the tilt angle manually through the year?

It's only worth the effort on systems where seasonal output actually matters for your use case — like off-grid setups sized around winter production. For a standard grid-tied rooftop system, the added complexity usually isn't worth the modest gain.
