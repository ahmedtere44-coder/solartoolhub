How long do solar panels actually last?

Warranties usually say 25 years — but that's not the same as "stops working at 25 years." Here's what actually happens to a solar panel over its lifetime.

"25 years" gets thrown around so often in solar marketing that it's easy to assume panels simply stop producing power the day that warranty ends. That's not how it works. Understanding what actually happens to a panel over decades of use helps set realistic expectations — both for your own system and for anything you're told during a sales pitch.

What "25-year warranty" actually means

Most manufacturer warranties are performance warranties, not lifespan guarantees. A typical warranty promises that after 25 years, a panel will still produce at least 80–87% of its original rated output — not that it will fail or stop working entirely at that point. Panels don't have a hard expiration date; they gradually produce less power every year, in a process called degradation.

Understanding degradation rates

Modern crystalline silicon panels typically degrade at around 0.5% per year, though premium panels can be as low as 0.3%, and older or lower-quality panels can degrade faster. Over time, this adds up predictably:

YearOutput at 0.5%/year degradation
Year 1~99.5% of original rating
Year 10~95%
Year 25~88%
Year 40~80%

This is why a well-maintained panel from a reputable manufacturer can still be producing usable power well past its 25-year warranty period — it's simply generating somewhat less than it did on day one, not nothing at all.

What actually shortens a panel's real-world lifespan

Degradation is gradual and predictable, but a handful of external factors can accelerate wear or cause premature failure well before the expected timeline:

  • Heat — panels lose efficiency in extreme heat, and prolonged high temperatures accelerate the breakdown of internal materials over years of exposure.
  • Poor installation — improper mounting, inadequate ventilation behind panels, or water ingress from bad sealing can cause failures far earlier than expected.
  • Physical damage — hail, falling debris, or impact damage can crack cells and create hot spots that degrade a panel faster than normal aging.
  • Dust and dirt buildup — this doesn't damage the panel itself, but consistently reduces output if left uncleaned, especially in dry or desert climates (see the field note on our panel calculator page for more on this).
  • Manufacturing quality — cheaper, lower-tier panels are more prone to early micro-cracks and connection failures than panels from established manufacturers with a longer track record.

What happens after 25 years?

In most cases, nothing dramatic — the panel keeps producing power, just at a somewhat reduced output. Whether it's still worth keeping depends on your situation:

  • If the panel is still producing 80%+ of its original output and shows no physical damage, many systems simply keep running well beyond their warranty period.
  • If output has dropped significantly below what a warranty claim would guarantee, or if newer panels offer meaningfully better efficiency per square meter, replacement may make more financial sense than continuing to run older, lower-output equipment.
  • Other components — particularly the inverter — often need replacement well before the panels themselves, typically once around year 12–15 of a system's life.

A real-world example

Take a residential system installed 8 years ago, rated at 5kW when new. With a typical degradation rate of 0.5% per year, that system would be expected to still produce around 96% of its original rating today — roughly 4.8kW of capacity — assuming no physical damage and reasonably regular cleaning. Inspections of similar-aged systems generally confirm numbers in this range, which is exactly the point: gradual, predictable decline, not a sudden drop-off. A noticeably lower output than this after only 8 years is usually a sign of a specific issue — heavy dust buildup, a failing connection, or physical damage — rather than normal aging.

Frequently asked questions

Do solar panels need to be replaced after 25 years?

Not necessarily. Many panels continue producing meaningful power for 30–40 years. The 25-year figure is a warranty milestone, not an expiration date.

Does cleaning frequency affect panel lifespan?

Cleaning affects output more than long-term lifespan — a dirty panel still ages at roughly the same rate, but produces less power in the meantime. In dusty climates, regular cleaning is more about maximizing current output than extending the panel's life.

Is it worth buying panels with a longer warranty?

Generally yes, since a longer or stronger performance warranty is usually a signal of manufacturing quality and confidence in the product — though it's worth checking the manufacturer's track record and financial stability too, since a warranty is only useful if the company is still around to honor it decades later.
