Annual maintenance checklist

A yearly systematic pass over the parts of a solar system most likely to quietly degrade — connections, cables, grounding, and overall performance.

Solar systems have very few moving parts, which is exactly why they get neglected — there's no oil to change, no filter to replace, nothing that obviously demands attention on a schedule. But connections loosen, corrosion creeps in, and output quietly drifts downward long before anything fails outright. A once-a-year systematic check catches almost all of it while it's still a five-minute fix.

Visual & mechanical checks

  • Panel surfaces free of heavy soiling, bird droppings, or debris buildup — see our cleaning guide for safe methods
  • Racking and mounting hardware still tight — check for any hardware loosened by a year of thermal cycling and wind
  • No new physical damage: cracked glass, delaminating panel edges, or bent frames
  • Cable runs still properly secured, with no new abrasion points, UV-damaged insulation, or rodent damage
  • Enclosures (combiner boxes, inverter housing) still sealed, with no water staining or visible corrosion inside

Electrical checks

  • Terminal connections torque-checked — not just visually inspected, since a loose connection often looks fine until it's actually tested
  • Grounding continuity re-verified — see our full grounding inspection guide for the process
  • String voltages measured and compared against the previous year's baseline, not just the datasheet spec — a gradual drop year over year is a useful early warning sign
  • Overcurrent protection devices (fuses, breakers) inspected for any signs of heat damage or discoloration
Field note

During one routine check, I noticed the full string voltage wasn't matching the expected value. The cause turned out to be a fault inside one panel's junction box — after replacing that panel, the string returned to normal and produced its full rated voltage and current.

Performance checks

  • Compare actual production against expected output for the season — a system quietly underperforming by 10-15% often has one identifiable cause (soiling, a failing panel, shading growth from nearby trees) rather than uniform aging
  • Check inverter/monitoring logs for any recurring fault codes since the last check — see our fault code guide if anything unfamiliar shows up
  • For battery-based systems, check that batteries are still reaching full charge and holding capacity consistent with the past year

Seasonal considerations

SeasonWhat to watch for
SpringPollen and debris buildup after winter; a good time for a full cleaning pass
SummerPeak thermal stress on components; verify cooling/ventilation isn't blocked
AutumnFalling leaves and growing shade from nearby trees as they mature
WinterSnow loading on racking, and reduced production that shouldn't be mistaken for a fault

Frequently asked questions

Is once a year really enough?

For most residential systems in moderate climates, yes — solar systems age slowly. Coastal, highly humid, or extreme-weather locations may benefit from a check every 6 months instead.

Can I do this checklist myself, or do I need a professional?

Visual checks and basic voltage/continuity testing are within reach for a reasonably confident DIYer with a multimeter — see our multimeter testing guide. Ground resistance testing and anything involving opening inverter enclosures is best left to a qualified technician.

What's the single most valuable check on this list?

Comparing actual production against your own historical baseline catches problems that a purely visual inspection misses entirely — degradation and loose connections often show up in the numbers well before they're visible to the eye.
