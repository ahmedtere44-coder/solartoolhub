The complete off-grid solar system guide

Everything you need to plan an off-grid solar system — how it works, the components involved, and a step-by-step sizing path with a free calculator for each stage. Start at the top, or jump to the tool you need.

Going off-grid means building a power system that stands completely on its own — no utility connection to lean on. That makes it one of the most rewarding solar projects to plan, and also the one where correct sizing matters most, because every component has to carry its share with no grid as a safety net. This guide walks through the whole picture, then points you to the exact calculator for each decision.

How an off-grid system works

An off-grid system follows a simple energy flow: the solar panels generate DC power during daylight, a charge controller regulates that power to safely charge a battery bank, and an inverter converts the stored DC into the AC electricity your appliances use — day or night. Because there's no grid to absorb surplus or cover shortfalls, the battery bank is the heart of the system, storing enough energy to carry you through nights and cloudy days. The sizing order (and why it matters)

The sequence above isn't arbitrary — each step feeds the next. Your daily load determines how big a battery bank you need. The battery capacity and your local sun hours set the size of the solar array. The array's output current sizes the charge controller. And your peak simultaneous load sizes the inverter. Skip ahead or guess at the load, and every downstream number is off. That's why "how much do you actually use?" is always the first question.

Wiring and protection

Once the main components are sized, the cables between them have to be sized too — undersized wiring wastes energy as heat and can be a fire risk. Use the cable calculator to pick the right conductor size for each run, and the voltage drop calculator to confirm an existing cable stays within safe limits. For panels wired in series, the string sizing calculator keeps your array voltage inside the controller's window.

Battery bank: the part to get right

In an off-grid system the battery bank is usually the biggest cost and the component most sensitive to correct sizing. Too small and you'll run out of power on cloudy days; too large and you've overspent on capacity you rarely use. Depth of discharge and chemistry make a huge difference — lithium delivers far more usable energy than lead-acid of the same rating. To see how long a given bank will actually last on a specific load, use the battery runtime calculator.

Keeping it running

An off-grid system is only as good as its upkeep, since there's no grid to mask problems. Regular checks catch loose connections and declining batteries before they leave you in the dark — our annual maintenance checklist covers what to inspect and when.

Frequently asked questions

What do I need for an off-grid solar system?

Five core components: solar panels, a charge controller, a battery bank, an inverter, and correctly sized cables with protection. Sizing starts from your daily energy consumption and works outward to each component.

How do I size an off-grid solar system?

Add up your daily watt-hours, size the battery for one to three days of storage, size the array to replace that energy during your sun hours, size the charge controller to the array current, and size the inverter to your peak load. Use the calculators above, or the all-in-one wizard.

Do off-grid systems need batteries?

Yes — with no grid to fall back on, a battery bank is essential for power at night and during cloudy weather. It's usually the most expensive part, so accurate sizing matters most here.
