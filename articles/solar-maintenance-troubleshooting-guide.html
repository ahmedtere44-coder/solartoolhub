Solar maintenance & troubleshooting guide

Keep your system producing at its best and know exactly what to do when something goes wrong. This guide brings together every maintenance routine and troubleshooting walkthrough on the site, organized so you can jump straight to what you need.

Solar systems are famously low-maintenance — no moving parts, no fuel, no daily attention. But "low-maintenance" isn't "no-maintenance." Panels get dirty, connections loosen, batteries age, and inverters occasionally throw a fault. The good news is that keeping a system healthy takes only a light, predictable routine, and most problems have clear, diagnosable causes. This guide organizes it all into two halves: routine care, and troubleshooting when something's off.

Prevention starts at installation

The easiest problems to fix are the ones you never create. A careful installation and a proper pre-startup check prevent most of the faults you'd otherwise troubleshoot later. Two guides cover this ground: common installation mistakes shows what to avoid, and the pre-commissioning checklist catches errors before the system is ever energized.

A simple maintenance rhythm

If you remember nothing else, this rhythm keeps almost any system healthy: clean the panels whenever they look dirty, glance over the wiring and mounting every few months, and once a year run the full annual checklist. In hot, dusty regions, cleaning matters even more — our guide on solar panels in hot climates explains why heat and dust together are so costly to output.

Frequently asked questions

How do I maintain a solar power system?

Keep panels clean, check electrical connections, and catch problems early. Clean when dirty, do a quick visual check a few times a year, and run a thorough inspection annually. With few moving parts, this light routine is usually enough.

How do I troubleshoot a solar system that isn't working?

Start at the inverter display and note any fault code. If output is low rather than zero, test panels with a multimeter, check for dirt or shading, and inspect connections for corrosion. Working from the inverter back to the panels isolates most problems.

How often should solar panels be inspected?

A quick visual check every few months and a thorough inspection once a year suits most systems. Cleaning frequency depends on your environment — dusty regions need it more often.
